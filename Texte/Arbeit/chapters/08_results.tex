\chapter{Results}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter presents the results of the experiments described in Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments}. All experiments target the prediction of the stream function~$\psi$ for Stokes flow around settling crystals. The evaluation covers training convergence, quantitative error metrics on a held-out evaluation set, and qualitative inspection of predicted flow fields. Results are organized by experiment; within each experiment, FNO and U-Net are presented separately and then compared.

% ============================================================================
\section{Experiment~1: Single-Crystal Baseline}
\label{sec:results:exp1}
% ============================================================================

Experiment~1 establishes a per-architecture baseline on the simplest configuration: single-crystal flows ($n = 1$). The sole free parameter across the four configurations is the learning rate; all other hyperparameters are fixed at the architecture defaults (see Section~\ref{sec:experiments:setup}). The four configurations are referred to as \emph{Exp-1.1} through \emph{Exp-1.4}, with learning rates $10^{-3}$, $5\times10^{-3}$, $10^{-4}$, and $5\times10^{-4}$.

\subsection{U-Net – Training Behavior}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:unet:training}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:unet_training_exp1} shows the training and validation loss curves for the four U-Net configurations (batch size~16, 180~epochs each). The curves of Exp-1.1 ($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-3}$) and Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) exhibit pronounced oscillations in validation loss, particularly in the early epochs, suggesting that the respective learning rates are too large for stable U-Net convergence without gradient clipping. This instability is consistent with the effective-learning-rate (ELR) theory of \textcite{alimehmeti-gopel_weight_2024}: above a critical learning rate, ELR ratios between BatchNorm-normalized layers can temporarily diverge, destabilizing convergence. Despite this, Exp-1.1 still reaches a best validation relative $L_2$ error of $3.5\%$ at epoch~173. Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) shows the smoothest convergence and achieves the lowest best-checkpoint validation error ($3.0\%$ at epoch~179). Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$) reaches $3.4\%$ at epoch~163, closely following Exp-1.3. Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) is the weakest configuration at $4.8\%$, likely due to the large learning rate causing it to overshoot the optimum early on.

For batch size~8, all four configurations cluster tightly in the range $5.5$–$6.5\%$ on~$\psi$, with Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) achieving the best result ($5.5\%$ / $8.5\%$). The velocity errors remain roughly $1.5$–$2\times$ larger than the $\psi$ errors, which is a clear improvement over a plain-MSE training baseline, where the ratio was approximately $3\times$ (see Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp1:unet:hf}). The gradient term in the composite loss explicitly penalizes errors in the derivatives of $\hat\psi$ and thereby suppresses high-frequency residuals that would otherwise be amplified by the finite-difference differentiation step.

For batch size~16, the composite loss leads to substantially higher evaluation errors ($13.5$–$24.0\%$) compared to the plain-MSE baseline ($8.6$–$11.2\%$). This suggests that the additional loss terms introduce a training difficulty for the larger batch size, possibly due to noisier gradient estimates from the spatial derivative computation combined with the already less stable U-Net convergence at bs\,16 (Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp1:unet:training}). Since bs\,8 consistently outperforms bs\,16 across all configurations, the remainder of this chapter focuses on bs\,8 results for the U-Net.

\subsection{U-Net – High-Frequency Residuals in the Velocity Field}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:unet:hf}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

When trained with plain MSE loss (without the gradient penalty term), the U-Net yields evaluation errors of approximately $5.2\%$ on~$\psi$ and $\approx 16\%$ on the derived velocity field for the best bs\,8 configuration — a velocity-to-$\psi$ error ratio of roughly $3\times$. Since the velocity field is obtained by finite-difference differentiation of~$\hat\psi$, high-frequency residuals in the predicted stream function are amplified by the differentiation step, disproportionately increasing the velocity error. This explains why the plain-MSE ratio exceeds the corresponding FNO ratio ($\approx 2\times$) even when the $\psi$ errors are similar: the FNO's spectral architecture naturally suppresses high-frequency components above the retained mode count ($k_{\max} = 16$), whereas the U-Net places no such constraint on the spatial frequency content of its output.

The adoption of the composite loss reduces the velocity-to-$\psi$ ratio to $1.5$–$2\times$, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:unet_metrics_exp1}, confirming that the gradient penalty term effectively suppresses the high-frequency artefacts responsible for the inflated velocity errors. For the best bs\,16 configuration ($8.6\%$ $\psi$, $\approx 26\%$ velocity under plain MSE), the ratio likewise improves with composite loss, although the absolute error level remains substantially higher than for bs\,8.

\subsection{Comparison: FNO vs.\ U-Net}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:comparison}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Table~\ref{tab:comparison_exp1} compares the best results from each architecture in Experiment~1 (FNO: 100~epochs; U-Net: 180~epochs, composite loss). For FNO, Exp-1.4 (bs\,16) achieves a mean relative $L_2$ error of $2.26\%$ on~$\psi$ and $4.56\%$ on the derived velocity field after only 100~epochs; with bs\,8 the best result is Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) at $1.70\%$ / $3.07\%$. The best U-Net result is Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$, bs\,8) at $5.5\%$ on~$\psi$ and $8.5\%$ on velocity after 180~epochs — a factor of roughly $3.2\times$ above the FNO bs\,16 optimum on~$\psi$. This highlights a structural advantage of the FNO: it reaches substantially lower error levels in fewer training epochs, suggesting that the spectral architecture is better suited for this class of problems both in terms of accuracy and training efficiency.

Overall, the FNO converges to substantially lower error levels, suggesting a structural advantage of the spectral architecture for smooth, globally-structured Stokes flow fields around a single crystal. The adoption of the composite loss for the U-Net partially closes the gap in velocity accuracy, and confirms that the gradient term reduces high-frequency residuals regardless of architecture. Both models benefit from the stream-function output representation: the divergence RMS of all FNO predictions is on the order of $10^{-26}$, confirming that incompressibility is preserved analytically irrespective of prediction accuracy.

\subsection{Spatial Error Analysis: Crystal Position vs.\ Prediction Error}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:spatial}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To examine whether the prediction accuracy depends on the location of the crystal within the computational domain, the relative $L_2$ error on~$\psi$ was compared against the Euclidean distance of the crystal centre from the domain centre across all held-out evaluation samples. The analysis used the best-performing configurations for batch size~16: Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$) for the FNO and Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) for the U-Net.

The FNO errors ($1.2$–$2.6\%$) show no systematic positional bias; the regression slope is nearly flat, indicating that the spectral architecture generalizes uniformly across the domain for the single-crystal case. The U-Net errors are substantially higher ($7.5$–$23\%$) with a mildly positive regression slope, suggesting a weak tendency for larger errors when the crystal is located further from the centre. However, with only ten evaluation samples the statistical significance of this trend is limited, and the dominant factor is the overall higher error level of the U-Net rather than a systematic positional effect.

\section{Experiment~2: Multi-Crystal Generalization}
\label{sec:results:exp2}
% ============================================================================

Experiment~2 trains both models on samples with $n \in \{1, \ldots, 10\}$ crystals and evaluates each on all training crystal counts as well as on unseen higher counts up to $n = 25$. The U-Net is trained for 180~epochs; the FNO is trained for 100~epochs.

Rather than fixing a single hyperparameter combination for all architectures, the configurations for Experiment~2 are derived directly from the Experiment~1 evaluation results: for each architecture and batch size, the learning rate that achieved the lowest held-out evaluation error in Experiment~1 is carried over.

For the \textbf{FNO}, this gives lr~$= 5\times10^{-4}$ for batch size~16 (Exp-1.4, $2.26\%$) and lr~$= 5\times10^{-3}$ for batch size~8 (Exp-1.2, $1.70\%$). The two batch-size variants represent different trade-offs: the larger batch with the lower learning rate offers more stable gradient estimates in the more complex multi-crystal loss landscape, while the smaller batch with the higher learning rate extrapolates the best-performing single-crystal setting directly.

For the \textbf{U-Net}, all batch-size\,8 configurations cluster within a narrow range in Experiment~1 ($5.5$–$6.5\%$), so the choice of learning rate is less critical. However, the configuration with the lowest validation error (lr~$= 10^{-4}$, Exp-1.3, $2.68\%$ val) shows a notable gap to its evaluation performance ($6.5\%$ eval), which may indicate that the best checkpoint is overfit to the relatively small validation set rather than genuinely better. For this reason, lr~$= 10^{-3}$ is preferred for batch size~8 — it converges quickly, avoids this discrepancy, and is well-suited to the multi-crystal setting where the best checkpoint tends to appear early in training (Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp2:unet:training}). For batch size~16, lr~$= 10^{-4}$ achieved the best evaluation result in Experiment~1 ($13.5\%$, Exp-1.3) and is retained here, though bs\,16 consistently underperforms bs\,8 across all U-Net configurations.

\section{Experiment~3: Stress-Testing Generalization Limits}
\label{sec:results:exp3}
% ============================================================================

Experiment~3 trains both models on configurations with up to $n = 25$ crystals and evaluates each on substantially more complex scenes with up to $n = 84$ crystals to probe the limits of out-of-distribution generalization. The U-Net is trained for 180~epochs; the FNO is trained for 100~epochs.

The hyperparameter configurations follow the same rationale as Experiment~2: the learning rates identified as best-performing for each architecture and batch size in Experiment~1 are used without modification. For the FNO, this means lr~$= 5\times10^{-4}$ with batch size~16 and lr~$= 5\times10^{-3}$ with batch size~8. For the U-Net, lr~$= 10^{-3}$ is used with batch size~8 and lr~$= 10^{-4}$ with batch size~16. The extended training distribution ($n \leq 25$ instead of $n \leq 10$) increases the complexity of each training batch but does not change the reasoning behind the choice of learning rate and batch size.

% ============================================================================

Experiment~4 evaluates the crystal-size generalization of both models by testing them on crystals whose radii lie outside the training distribution. The FNO model used is the best-performing Exp-1 configuration (Exp-1.4, bs\,16, lr~$=5\times10^{-4}$), trained on the standard single-crystal distribution. The U-Net model is the best-performing Exp-2 configuration (bs\,8, lr~$=10^{-3}$), trained on multi-crystal configurations with $n \leq 10$. Each evaluation set contains ten single-crystal samples ($n = 1$). Two size regimes are evaluated: \emph{small crystals}, whose radius is substantially below the training distribution, and \emph{large crystals}, whose radius is substantially above the training distribution.

For small crystals, both architectures degrade moderately: the FNO reaches $12.1\%$ on~$\psi$ and the U-Net $10.8\%$, compared to their respective in-distribution errors of $2.3\%$ (FNO, Exp-1) and $\sim 28\%$ (U-Net, Exp-2, $n=1$). For large crystals, both models degrade severely. The FNO reaches $71.3\%$ on~$\psi$ and $67.7\%$ on velocity — a near-complete failure of the prediction. The U-Net performs markedly better at $44.9\%$ on~$\psi$ and $50.7\%$ on velocity, though absolute errors remain large. Notably, this is one of the few scenarios in this thesis where the U-Net outperforms the FNO.

\subsection{Discussion of Size Generalization}
\label{ssec:results:exp4:discussion}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The asymmetry between small- and large-crystal degradation is consistent with the spectral properties of both architectures. A larger crystal occupies a greater fraction of the computational domain and induces steeper velocity gradients near its boundary. For the FNO, these boundary-layer features require high-frequency Fourier content that exceeds the retained mode count ($k_{\max} = 16$), leading to the observed catastrophic failure. The U-Net, with its spatially local convolution kernels and skip connections, is somewhat better equipped to represent sharp spatial gradients at new scales, explaining its more moderate degradation.

For small crystals, the flow field is dominated by an extended, globally smooth region far from the crystal boundary, which both architectures can approximate reasonably well regardless of the specific crystal radius.
