\section{Experiment~1: Single-Crystal Baseline}
\label{sec:results:exp1}
% ============================================================================

Experiment~1 establishes a per-architecture baseline on the simplest configuration: single-crystal flows ($n = 1$). The two free parameters varied across configurations are the learning rate and the batch size; all other hyperparameters are fixed at the architecture defaults (see Section~\ref{sec:experiments:setup}). Four learning rates are evaluated—$10^{-3}$, $5\times10^{-3}$, $10^{-4}$, and $5\times10^{-4}$—each in combination with batch sizes of 8 and~16, yielding eight configurations per architecture. The four learning-rate configurations are referred to as \emph{Exp-1.1} through \emph{Exp-1.4}.

For batch size~16 (bs\,16), Exp-1.4 ($\mathrm{lr}=5\times10^{-4}$) yields the lowest errors across all metrics after 100~epochs, achieving a mean relative $L_2$ error of $1.62\%$ on~$\psi$ and $3.44\%$ on the derived velocity field.
Exp-1.1 ($10^{-3}$) and Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) follow at $2.06\%$ / $3.02\%$ and $2.31\%$ / $4.15\%$ respectively, while Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) remains the worst at $5.10\%$ / $8.18\%$: at this low learning rate, the model converges too slowly to reach a competitive optimum within the available 100~epochs.
For batch size~8 (bs\,8), Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) achieves the best bs\,8 result with $2.07\%$ / $4.13\%$, but does not surpass the best bs\,16 result (Exp-1.4: $1.62\%$ / $3.44\%$). Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$) follows at $2.61\%$ / $3.82\%$, Exp-1.1 ($10^{-3}$) reaches $4.18\%$ / $4.89\%$, and Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) again lags behind at $5.85\%$ / $10.56\%$, consistent with the pattern observed for bs\,16.

A notable pattern is that the optimal learning rate shifts when the batch size changes: while lr~$= 5\times10^{-4}$ is best for bs\,16, lr~$= 5\times10^{-3}$ is best for bs\,8. This is consistent with the linear scaling rule of \textcite{goyal2018accuratelargeminibatchsgd}, which prescribes scaling the learning rate proportionally to the batch size: halving the batch size should be accompanied by halving the learning rate to keep the effective step size constant. \textcite{smith2018dontdecaylearningrate} provide a complementary perspective, showing that the ratio of learning rate to batch size governs the noise scale of stochastic gradient descent; keeping this ratio constant is equivalent to interchanging batch size reduction and learning-rate decay. In our setting, the factor-of-two difference in batch size is reflected approximately by a factor-of-two difference in optimal learning rate ($5\times10^{-4}$ vs.\ $5\times10^{-3}$ would exceed the linear prediction, but the FNO's gradient clipping and the non-convex loss landscape introduce additional confounders that can shift the optimal operating point).

\subsection{U-Net – Training Behavior}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:unet:training}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:unet_training_exp1} shows the training and validation loss curves for the four U-Net configurations (bs\,16, 180~epochs each). The curves of Exp-1.1 ($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-3}$) and Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) exhibit pronounced oscillations in validation loss, particularly in the early epochs, suggesting that the respective learning rates are too large for stable U-Net convergence without gradient clipping. This instability is consistent with the effective-learning-rate (ELR) theory of \textcite{alimehmeti-gopel_weight_2024}: above a critical learning rate, ELR ratios between BatchNorm-normalized layers can temporarily diverge, destabilizing convergence. Despite this, Exp-1.1 still reaches a best validation relative $L_2$ error of $3.5\%$ at epoch~173. Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) shows the smoothest convergence and achieves the lowest best-checkpoint validation relative $L_2$ error ($3.0\%$ at epoch~179). Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$) reaches $3.4\%$ (validation relative $L_2$ error) at epoch~163, closely following Exp-1.3. Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) is the weakest configuration at $4.8\%$ (validation relative $L_2$ error), likely due to the large learning rate causing it to overshoot the optimum early on. For bs\,8 (right panel), the validation curves are generally smoother, with best-checkpoint validation relative $L_2$ errors in the range $2.7$–$3.4\%$ across all four configurations—a more uniform spread than for bs\,16, suggesting that the smaller batch size stabilizes training independently of the learning rate choice.

For bs\,8, all four configurations cluster tightly in the range $5.5$–$6.5\%$ on the stream function~$\psi$, with Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) achieving the best result ($5.5\%$ on~$\psi$ / $8.5\%$ on the derived velocity field). The gradient term in the composite loss explicitly penalizes errors in the spatial derivatives of the predicted stream function~$\hat{\psi}$ and thereby suppresses high-frequency residuals that would otherwise be amplified by the finite-difference differentiation step.

For bs\,16, the composite loss leads to substantially higher evaluation errors ($13.5$–$24.0\%$ on~$\psi$) compared to the plain-MSE baseline ($8.6$–$11.2\%$; these values are reported in Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp1:unet:hf} for the bs\,16 configurations). This suggests that the additional loss terms introduce a training difficulty for the larger batch size, possibly due to higher-variance gradient estimates from the spatial derivative computation—the stochastic noise in the gradient signal increases when fewer samples per batch are averaged—combined with the already less stable U-Net convergence at bs\,16 (Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp1:unet:training}). Since bs\,8 consistently outperforms bs\,16 across all configurations, the remainder of this chapter focuses on bs\,8 results for the U-Net.

\subsection{U-Net – High-Frequency Residuals in the Velocity Field}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:unet:hf}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

When trained with plain-MSE loss (without the gradient penalty term), the U-Net yields evaluation errors of approximately $5.2\%$ on~$\psi$ and $\approx 16\%$ on the derived velocity field for the best bs\,8 configuration (note: the $8.6$–$11.2\%$ range cited in Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp1:unet:metrics} refers to plain-MSE errors on~$\psi$ for the bs\,16 configurations, a different metric and batch size)—a velocity-to-$\psi$ error ratio of roughly $3\times$. Since the velocity field is obtained by finite-difference differentiation of~$\hat\psi$, high-frequency residuals in the predicted stream function are amplified by the differentiation step, disproportionately increasing the velocity error. This explains why the plain-MSE ratio exceeds the corresponding FNO ratio ($\approx 2\times$) even when the $\psi$ errors are similar: the FNO's spectral architecture naturally suppresses high-frequency components above the retained mode count ($k_{\max} = 16$) \parencite{qin_toward_2024}, whereas the U-Net places no such constraint on the spatial frequency content of its output.

The adoption of the composite loss reduces the velocity-to-$\psi$ ratio to $1.5$–$2\times$, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:unet_metrics_exp1}, confirming that the gradient penalty term effectively suppresses the high-frequency artefacts responsible for the inflated velocity errors. For bs\,16 configurations, the ratio likewise improves with composite loss, although the absolute error level remains substantially higher than for bs\,8.

\label{ssec:results:exp1:comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison_exp1} compares the best results from each architecture in Experiment~1 (FNO: 100~epochs; U-Net: 180~epochs, composite loss). For FNO, Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$, bs\,16) achieves a mean relative $L_2$ error of $1.62\%$ on~$\psi$ and $3.44\%$ on the derived velocity field; with bs\,8 the best result is Exp-1.2 ($5\times10^{-3}$) at $2.07\%$ / $4.13\%$, which does not surpass the bs\,16 optimum. This highlights a structural advantage of the FNO: it reaches substantially lower error levels, suggesting that the spectral architecture is better suited for this class of problems.

Overall, the FNO converges to substantially lower error levels, suggesting a structural advantage of the spectral architecture for smooth, globally-structured Stokes flow fields around a single crystal. The adoption of the composite loss for the U-Net partially closes the gap in velocity accuracy, and confirms that the gradient term reduces high-frequency residuals regardless of architecture. Both models benefit from the stream-function output representation: the divergence RMS of all FNO predictions is on the order of $10^{-26}$, confirming that incompressibility is preserved analytically irrespective of prediction accuracy.

\subsection{Spatial Error Analysis: Crystal Position vs.\ Prediction Error}
\label{ssec:results:exp1:spatial}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To examine whether the prediction accuracy depends on the location of the crystal within the computational domain, the relative $L_2$ error on~$\psi$ was compared against the Euclidean distance of the crystal centre from the domain centre across all held-out evaluation samples. The analysis used the best-performing configurations for batch size~16: Exp-1.4 ($5\times10^{-4}$) for the FNO and Exp-1.3 ($10^{-4}$) for the U-Net.

The FNO errors ($0.9$–$2.3\%$) show no systematic positional bias; the regression slope is nearly flat, indicating that the spectral architecture generalizes uniformly across the domain for the single-crystal case. The U-Net errors are substantially higher ($7.5$–$23\%$) with a mildly positive regression slope, suggesting a weak tendency for larger errors when the crystal is located further from the centre. However, with only ten evaluation samples the statistical significance of this trend is limited, and the dominant factor is the overall higher error level of the U-Net rather than a systematic positional effect.

\section{Experiment~2: Multi-Crystal Generalization}
\label{sec:results:exp2}
% ============================================================================

Experiment~2 trains both models on samples with $n \in \{1, \ldots, 10\}$ crystals and evaluates each on all training crystal counts as well as on unseen higher counts up to $n = 25$. The U-Net is trained for 180~epochs; the FNO for 100~epochs.

Rather than fixing a single hyperparameter combination for all architectures, the configurations for Experiment~2 are derived directly from the Experiment~1 evaluation results: for each architecture and batch size, the learning rate that achieved the lowest held-out evaluation error in Experiment~1 is carried over.

For the FNO, this gives lr~$= 5\times10^{-4}$ for batch size~16 (Exp-1.4, $1.62\%$) and lr~$= 5\times10^{-3}$ for batch size~8 (Exp-1.2, $2.07\%$). The two batch size variants represent different trade-offs: the larger batch with the lower learning rate offers more stable gradient estimates in the more complex multi-crystal loss landscape, while the smaller batch with the higher learning rate extrapolates the best-performing single-crystal setting directly.

For the U-Net, all batch size\,8 configurations cluster within a narrow range in Experiment~1 ($5.5$–$6.5\%$), so the choice of learning rate is less critical. However, the configuration with the lowest validation error (lr~$= 10^{-4}$, Exp-1.3, $2.68\%$ val) shows a notable gap to its evaluation performance ($6.5\%$ eval), which may indicate that the best checkpoint is overfit to the relatively small validation set rather than genuinely better. For this reason, lr~$= 10^{-3}$ (Exp-1.1) is preferred for batch size~8—it converges quickly, avoids this discrepancy, and is well-suited to the multi-crystal setting where the best checkpoint tends to appear early in training (Section~\ref{ssec:results:exp2:unet:training}). For batch size~16, lr~$= 10^{-4}$ achieved the best evaluation result in Experiment~1 ($13.5\%$, Exp-1.3) and is retained here, though bs\,16 consistently underperforms bs\,8 across all U-Net configurations.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{U-Net – Training Behavior}
\label{ssec:results:exp2:unet:training}

The systematic near-boundary underestimation is a characteristic consequence of spectral truncation — increasing $k_{\max}$ beyond 16 would reduce these near-field residuals, but at a proportional increase in computational cost. The robustness of the qualitative output across both batch size configurations further supports the conclusion that the learned spectral representation is insensitive to moderate changes in training hyperparameters.

Experiment~3 trains both models on configurations with up to $n = 25$ crystals and evaluates each on substantially more complex scenes with up to $n = 84$ crystals to probe the limits of out-of-distribution generalization. The U-Net is trained for 180~epochs; the FNO for 100~epochs.

\subsection{Discussion of Size Generalization}
\label{ssec:results:exp4:discussion}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The asymmetry between small- and large-crystal degradation is consistent with the spectral properties of both architectures. A larger crystal occupies a greater fraction of the computational domain and induces steeper velocity gradients near its boundary. For the FNO, these boundary-layer features require high-frequency Fourier content that exceeds the retained mode count ($k_{\max} = 16$), leading to the observed catastrophic failure. The U-Net, with its spatially local convolution kernels and skip connections, is somewhat better equipped to represent sharp spatial gradients at new scales, explaining its more moderate degradation.

For small crystals, the flow field is dominated by an extended, globally smooth region far from the crystal boundary, which both architectures can approximate reasonably well regardless of the specific crystal radius.

\subsection{Effect of Size Conditioning}
\label{ssec:results:exp4:conditioning}

Compared to the fixed-radius baseline (FNO Exp-1.4, trained on $r = 0.05$ only; Table~\ref{tab:metrics_exp4}), the variable-radius model avoids the catastrophic failure observed for large crystals ($71.3\%$ on $\psi$), demonstrating that including size variability in training data substantially improves robustness to geometric scale changes. \section{Spectral Analysis of FNO Prediction Errors}
\label{sec:results:spectral}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Since the FNO operates by parameterizing the integral kernel in the Fourier domain and explicitly retaining only the lowest $k_{\max} = 16$ modes in each spatial direction \parencite{li_fourier_2021}, a natural question is whether the prediction errors are concentrated at wavenumbers beyond the truncation boundary. To investigate this, the normalized 2D power spectrum of the residual $\hat\psi - \psi$ is computed for each evaluation sample as $P(\mathbf{k}) = N^{-2}|\mathcal{F}\{\hat\psi - \psi\}(\mathbf{k})|^2$, averaged across all samples within each experiment, and summarized via a radial average $\bar{P}(k)$. The relative error fraction per wavenumber, $r(k) = \bar{P}_{\mathrm{residual}}(k) / \bar{P}_{\mathrm{truth}}(k)$, quantifies what fraction of signal power at each frequency is explained by the prediction error.

Figure~\ref{fig:freq_spectrum_2d} shows the mean 2D power spectra of the ground truth and the residual for all four experiments, zoomed to $k \in [-64, 64]$ to make the FNO truncation boundary (cyan dashed box, $k = \pm 16$) visible. The ground truth spectra are dominated by low-frequency content: virtually all energy is concentrated near the origin, consistent with the smooth, globally structured nature of Stokes flow. The cross-shaped pattern along the $k_x = 0$ and $k_z = 0$ axes reflects the periodic boundary conditions of the simulation domain. The residual spectra share the same qualitative structure, confirming that the prediction errors are distributed similarly to the signal itself rather than being concentrated at high wavenumbers.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../Ergebnisse/Bilder_Arbeit/fig_freq_spectrum_2d.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Mean 2D power spectra (log\textsubscript{10} scale) of the ground truth $\psi$ (top row)
             and the residual $\hat\psi - \psi$ (bottom row) for all four experiments (bs\,=\,16).}
             The cyan dashed box marks the FNO truncation boundary at $k = \pm 16$.
             Spectra are averaged over all held-out evaluation samples per experiment and
             zoomed to $k \in [-64,\,64]$.}
    \label{fig:freq_spectrum_2d}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:freq_error_ratio} quantifies this observation through the relative error fraction $r(k)$. Across all four experiments, $r(k)$ is approximately constant over the full displayed wavenumber range and remains well below $10^{-2}$ at every frequency—both inside and outside the FNO truncation boundary. No systematic increase is observed for $k > 16$, which would be the expected signature of truncation-induced error.

The same analysis applied to Experiment~3 samples grouped by crystal count ($n \leq 5$, $6$–$10$, $11$–$15$, $16$–$25$) yields indistinguishable curves, indicating that the spectral error distribution is not sensitive to the number of crystals present in the scene.
