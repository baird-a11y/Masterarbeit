\section{Geoscientific Context}

Crystal sedimentation in viscous magmatic fluids is a fundamental process governing the evolution, differentiation, and internal dynamics of magma reservoirs \parencite{martin_crystal_1988,martin_fluid-dynamical_1989}. During cooling, crystals nucleate and grow within the melt and begin to settle due to density contrasts between the solid and liquid phases. This settling process directly influences the thermal and chemical evolution of the system and contributes to the formation of cumulate layers that are preserved as macroscopic structures in plutonic bodies \parencite{weinstein_evolution_1988,verhoeven_numerical_2009}. Minerals such as olivine and plagioclase are known to settle at rates that permit efficient chemical differentiation: in basaltic chambers, residence times range from days to a few years for mafic minerals and up to several centuries for plagioclase \parencite{martin_crystal_1988,martin_fluid-dynamical_1989}.

Early work by \textcite{martin_crystal_1988} showed that crystal settling can occur even in vigorously convecting magma chambers, provided that the Stokes settling velocity exceeds characteristic convective flow velocities near the chamber floor. This finding called into question the long-held view that convection necessarily suppresses sedimentation, and emphasized the role of local particle--fluid interactions. A settling crystal perturbs the surrounding flow field by generating viscous boundary layers and wake structures that extend over several crystal diameters \parencite{happel_low_1983}. These flow perturbations are not confined to the immediate vicinity of a single particle but can modify the trajectories and settling behavior of neighboring crystals.

Subsequent experimental and numerical investigations \parencite{martin_fluid-dynamical_1989,weinstein_evolution_1988,verhoeven_numerical_2009,patocka_settling_2020,uhlmann_sedimentation_2014,penlou_experimental_2023,nissanka_dynamics_2023} revealed that such interactions may lead to asymmetric flow patterns, recirculation zones, and clustering phenomena. \textcite{weinstein_evolution_1988} further showed that the spatial distribution of deposited crystals is inherently asymmetric with respect to the geometry of the convection cell, offering a physical explanation for the non-uniform mineralogical layering commonly observed in plutonic rocks. As a consequence, even small variations in crystal geometry, spatial arrangement, or relative positioning can strongly affect collective settling dynamics and the development of such layering. Taken together, these results indicate that multi-crystal sedimentation is an inherently multiscale and interaction-dominated process, which places high demands on any numerical approach aimed at capturing the relevant physics.

\subsection{Physical Parameter Regime}

To ground the qualitative picture in concrete physical scales, it is useful to recall the relevant order-of-magnitude estimates. For mafic minerals such as olivine settling in a basaltic melt, typical parameter ranges are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Crystal radius:} $r \sim 0.1$--$10\,\text{mm}$ in basaltic chambers; larger crystals ($r \sim 1$--$5\,\text{cm}$) occur in slowly cooling plutonic bodies where crystal growth has proceeded over extended periods \parencite{martin_crystal_1988}.
    \item \textbf{Melt viscosity:} $\mu \sim 1$--$10^3\,\text{Pa\,s}$ for low-silica basaltic melts; significantly higher ($\mu \sim 10^6$--$10^{12}\,\text{Pa\,s}$) for rhyolitic compositions \parencite{martin_crystal_1988}. Crystals are effectively rigid relative to the melt ($\mu_\text{crystal}/\mu_\text{melt} \gtrsim 10^5$).
    \item \textbf{Density contrast:} olivine ($\rho \approx 3300\,\text{kg/m}^3$) vs.\ basaltic melt ($\rho \approx 2700\,\text{kg/m}^3$), yielding $\Delta\rho \approx 300$--$600\,\text{kg/m}^3$ \parencite{martin_crystal_1988}.
    \item \textbf{Stokes settling velocity:} from $v_s = \frac{2}{9}\frac{\Delta\rho\,g\,r^2}{\mu}$, a 1\,mm olivine crystal in a 10\,Pa\,s melt settles at $v_s \approx 10^{-5}\,\text{m/s}$ ($\approx 1\,\text{m/day}$); a 1\,cm crystal at $v_s \approx 10^{-3}\,\text{m/s}$ ($\approx 100\,\text{m/day}$).
    \item \textbf{Reynolds number:} $\mathrm{Re} = \rho\,v_s\,r/\mu \ll 1$ in all relevant cases (typically $\mathrm{Re} \sim 10^{-8}$--$10^{-4}$), confirming that the Stokes flow regime—in which inertial effects are negligible—is an appropriate physical approximation throughout the parameter range of interest.
\end{itemize}

In the normalized $[-1,1]^2$ simulation domain used in this thesis, the non-dimensionalized crystal radius $r = 0.05$ is consistent with a crystal that occupies approximately 5\% of the domain length. Hydrodynamic interaction effects become significant when crystal separation distances are comparable to several crystal radii \parencite{happel_low_1983}, which motivates the investigation of multi-crystal configurations.

Since the Stokes equations are linear and inertial effects are negligible ($\mathrm{Re} \ll 1$), the normalized flow field depends only on the geometric ratio $r/L$ and the relative crystal positions, not on the absolute values of $r$, $\mu$, or $\Delta\rho$ individually \parencite{happel_low_1983}. The crystal radius $r = 0.05$ in the normalized domain therefore represents a non-dimensional geometric parameter rather than a specific physical scale, and the surrogate model results are valid for any combination of physical parameters that yields $\mathrm{Re} \ll 1$ and the same geometric configuration. The size generalization study in Experiment~4, which varies $r$ in the range $0.01$--$0.1$, thus probes genuine changes in geometric topology—the ratio of crystal size to domain and inter-crystal spacing—rather than variations in any single dimensional quantity.

\section{Computational Challenges}

Accurate numerical simulation of multi-crystal sedimentation remains computationally demanding due to the strongly multiscale nature of crystal--fluid interactions. Any physically meaningful model needs to account for:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Microscale crystal boundaries}, where steep velocity gradients and localized shear layers develop,
    \item \textbf{Long-range hydrodynamic interactions} between multiple crystals mediated by the surrounding fluid, and
    \item \textbf{Domain-scale flow structures} that control the large-scale transport of crystals and melt.
\end{itemize}

The computational cost of resolving hydrodynamic interactions typically scales between $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ and $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ with the number of particles $N$ \parencite{ladd_numerical_nodate}. Numerical methods that explicitly couple particle and fluid dynamics such as Lattice Boltzmann discrete element methods (LBM-DEM) or immersed boundary approaches generally require each particle to be resolved by tens to hundreds of grid cells. Even in two-dimensional configurations, this leads to simulations with $10^5$-$10^6$ fluid nodes per particle \parencite{Li_IBM-LBM-DEM_2022,leonardi_coupled_2014}. For systems containing more than ten crystals, total grid sizes can easily exceed $10^7$--$10^8$ degrees of freedom, resulting in runtimes ranging from several hours to multiple days per simulation \parencite{Li_IBM-LBM-DEM_2022}.

Such computational demands make systematic parameter studies effectively infeasible. Investigating the influence of crystal size, viscosity contrast, particle number, or spatial arrangement would require thousands of high-fidelity simulations and thus exceed practical computational budgets. This is the main practical reason why surrogate models capable of approximating the underlying flow fields at a fraction of the computational cost, have attracted growing interest in recent years.

\section{Machine Learning as a Surrogate Modeling Strategy}

Machine learning has increasingly been applied to accelerate predictions in fluid dynamics, with the goal of replacing expensive numerical solvers with fast, trained approximations. Two architectural paradigms are of particular relevance to this work.

First, U-Net architectures \parencite{ronneberger_u-net_2015} leverage an encoder--decoder structure with skip connections to capture multiscale spatial features. Their ability to encode complex geometric information at multiple resolutions has made them a widely used choice for structured flow-field prediction \parencite{guo_convolutional_2016,thuerey_deep_2020,ribeiro_deepcfd_nodate,chen_u-net_nodate}.

Second, Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) \parencite{li_fourier_2021} learn mappings between function spaces by performing convolutions in the spectral domain via the Fast Fourier Transform. By operating in frequency space, FNOs can efficiently capture global, long-range dependencies in the solution field—a property that is particularly attractive for problems governed by elliptic operators such as the Stokes equations.

Both approaches have been shown to achieve speed-ups of several orders of magnitude over classical solvers, while maintaining acceptable accuracy across a range of flow problems \parencite{guo_convolutional_2016,thuerey_deep_2020,li_fourier_2021}. Beyond purely data-driven approaches, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) \parencite{raissi_physics_2017,raissi_physics-informed_2019,jin_nsfnets_2021} incorporate governing equations directly into the training objective, encouraging physically consistent predictions even when training data are scarce.

Taken together, these three families of methods—convolutional surrogates, neural operators, and physics-informed networks—span the design space from which the present thesis draws its central comparison. While systematic architecture comparisons \parencite{lu_comprehensive_2022} and generalization analyses \parencite{morimoto_generalization_2022} exist for standard benchmark problems, they have rarely been applied to physically structured, interaction-dominated problems such as multi-crystal Stokes flow with varying particle numbers. A comprehensive review of the relevant literature is provided in Chapter~\ref{ch:related}, and the resulting methodological landscape motivates the central research question addressed in this thesis.

\section{Research Question and Hypothesis}

The central research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
\emph{To what extent can U-Net and Fourier Neural Operator architectures, trained on configurations containing up to a fixed maximal number of crystals ($N$), generalize to unseen sedimentation scenarios with varying particle numbers and spatial arrangements and which factors fundamentally limit this generalization capability?}
\end{quote}

This question is of direct relevance to geoscientific applications, as natural sedimentation processes rarely involve fixed particle counts or highly regular geometries \parencite{martin_crystal_1988,verhoeven_numerical_2009}. A practically useful surrogate model must therefore generalize across a wide 
range of spatial configurations, interaction regimes, and crystal numbers.

We hypothesize that an ML-based surrogate model can achieve such generalization provided that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the training data sufficiently sample the space of possible geometric configurations;
    \item the learning task incorporates a physically meaningful target representation such as the stream function $\psi$—which enforces incompressibility by construction and reduces the effective degrees of freedom of the problem;
    \item the network architecture captures the inherently multiscale character of crystal--fluid interactions, whether through spatially hierarchical feature extraction (U-Net) or through global spectral representations (FNO).
\end{enumerate}

The controlled comparison of U-Net and FNO architectures presented in this thesis is designed to explicitly test these hypotheses, to quantify the impact of architectural choice on predictive performance, and to identify which paradigm—local spatial convolutions or global spectral operations—is better suited for surrogate modeling of multi-crystal Stokes flow.

\section{Objectives and Scope of the Study}

\subsection{Primary Objective}
This study aims to quantitatively evaluate the generalization performance of U-Net- and FNO-based surrogate models across a range of crystal numbers and spatial arrangements on a fixed $256 \times 256$ computational grid, with a particular focus on identifying the conditions under which generalization breaks down and how the two architectural paradigms compare in this respect.

\subsection{Secondary Objective}
A further objective is to identify recurring prediction errors and characteristic failure patterns encountered when predicting previously unseen geometries, including symmetry-breaking artifacts, nonphysical divergence, and reduced accuracy near crystal boundaries.

\subsection{Tertiary Objective}
Finally, this work seeks to derive practical guidelines for the construction of surrogate models for multiphase Stokes flow in geophysical settings, with a focus on input representations, output variables, normalization strategies, and training methodologies.

\subsection{Scope Limitations}

To maintain computational feasibility while addressing the core research questions, this study is restricted to:

\begin{itemize}
    \item two-dimensional incompressible Stokes flow formulated in stream-function-vorticity form (a standard starting point for such problems that captures the essential physics of crystal--fluid interactions \parencite{weinstein_evolution_1988,verhoeven_numerical_2009}),
    \item rigid circular crystal geometries without collision or lubrication forces,
    \item steady-state flow fields defined on a fixed $256 \times 256$ grid,
    \item synthetic training data generated using the LaMEM code \parencite{Popov2013LaMEM},
    \item pre-generated training, validation, and evaluation datasets stored on disk and loaded during training to ensure reproducibility and consistent benchmarking across architectures.
\end{itemize}

These assumptions allow for a focused investigation of surrogate modeling strategies while preserving the essential physical characteristics of multi-crystal sedimentation dynamics.

\section{Structure of the Thesis}

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows.
Chapter~\ref{ch:related} reviews recent machine-learning-based surrogate models for fluid dynamics and situates the present work within the existing literature.
Chapter~\ref{ch:theory} introduces the physical and mathematical foundations of Stokes flow, the stream-function formulation, and the U-Net and FNO architectures employed for structured flow-field prediction.
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} describes the computational workflow employed in this thesis, including LaMEM data generation, stream-function computation, input construction, network architecture, and training and evaluation procedures.
Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation} details the technical realization of the surrogate modeling framework, covering the software environment, model implementations, data pipeline, and training infrastructure.
Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} presents the experimental setup, including the choice of hyperparameters, training configurations, and evaluation metrics used throughout the study.
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the numerical results obtained from the U-Net and FNO surrogate models across varying crystal numbers and spatial arrangements.
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} interprets the results in the context of the research questions and hypotheses, identifies key insights and limitations, and discusses implications for future surrogate modeling efforts in geophysical fluid dynamics.
Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusions} summarizes the main findings of the study, draws conclusions with respect to the research hypotheses, and outlines directions for future work.
